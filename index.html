Research supports the connection
between ADHD and impared
omega fatty acid balance

Individuals with ADHD often have a substantial deficiency of essential
fatty acids, as well as a marked omega-3/omega-6 ratio imbalance. This
is important because, omega fatty acid deficiencies are thought to
negatively affect nervous system structure and function, which, in turn,
may impact the regulation of crucial signaling molecules and
inflammatory pathways in the brain.

How does this occur
and what can we do?

The answer is in the genes. Specific inborn genetic differences found in many individuals with ADHD can reduce the functional efficiency of enzymes that serve as rate-limiting steps for fatty acid metabolism. 

FADS-1 and FADS-2 genes are found in a genetically “hot”region for ADHD (on the 11th chromosome).6,9-14

These genes code for desaturase enzymes (delta-5 and delta-6) that convert essential fatty acids to long-chain polyunsaturated fatty acids.

In ADHD, different alleles can result in different forms of these enzymes, which may be less efficient at processing long-chain omega fatty acids.9,15

These genetic differences
make it difficult to
rebalance omega-fatty
acid deficiencies in ADHD
patients through normal
diet modification alone.

Combining higher amounts of EPA with DHA and an optimal balance of GLA has been shown to support a proper inflammatory response
by balancing the production of metabolites that come from AA (arachidonic acid) and maintaining the supportive capabilities of EPA,
DHA, and GLA. This is important because long-chain omega fatty acid deficiencies are thought to negatively affect key brain functions.
This balance can not be addressed as efficiently with omega-3s alone.
